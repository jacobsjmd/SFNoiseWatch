Federal Regulation

At the national level, regulations limit the allowable sound levels produces by aircraft, railway and roadway vehicles and certain heavy equipment. For example, almost all aircraft in current operation must meet standards for runway takeoffs, landings, and sidelines, which depend on the aircraft's weight and its number of engines. Emissions standards have a number of shortcomings. Emissions standards exit for only a minority of sources of noise; they may not apply to equipment already in use; and they do not address the problem of cumulative emissions. For example, emissions standards for new highway and railway construction and operation typically allow small incremental increases in noise levels. Overtime, these permitted incremental increases become significant and health adverse.

State and Local Regulation

Thoughtful planning and building rules and their strict enforcement can mitigate many noise conflicts. Zoning codes can limit the incompatible proximal location of residential and commercial uses. Building code can require acoustical insulation to keep sound inside bars and clubs and to keep living and sleeping areas quite while adequately ventilated.

Enforcement of all noise laws is variable and often subjective. Even where laws exists, municipalities may not have sufficient or trained noise enforcement personnel, the ability to monitor noise compliance, or the willingness to pursue enforcement actions against noise transgressors.
